7 The Voice

Generally, the most prominent instruments in the orchestra should double the voice. These include (a) the highest woodwind, (b) the highest brass, and (c) the highest string.

Melody

Throughout this book, the words “voice” and “melody” are used synonymously. Although the voice doesn’t always sing the melody in an opera, in a tutti chord it almost always does.

Doubling

We have seen in a previous chapter how the most doubled chord-tone in a root-position triad is the root. When the voice is singing a chord-tone other than the root, however, that chord-tone is sometimes doubled just as many, if not more, times than the root.

Schumann, Genoveva (p. 224):

Reduction:

First, it must be noted that Schumann has a reputation for being a poor orchestrator, and this passage helps to show why. For one thing, there is a missing chord-tone in the middle register, and while gaps are common above the staff, gaps in the middle register are hardly ever seen.

There is also an over-abundance of the pitch D in this chord, which conflicts with the voice’s F. In fact, every brass instrument is playing a D at its loudest dynamic, a triple forte. This creates a difficult hurdle for the vocalist to overcome. And while Schumann deserves our sympathy for having to deal with natural brass instruments, which had more limitations than our modern counterparts, there is no doubt that this passage could have been orchestrated better.

Wagner, The Flying Dutchman (p. 96):

Reduction:

Many of our principles are in evidence here: the woodwinds fill in all the chord-tones in the upper register, the horns fill in the chord-tones below the woodwinds, the flutes play the highest notes, most of the doublings occur in the middle register, each pair of woodwinds is playing a consonant interval, and each section of the orchestra is playing a complete chord.

If this orchestration has a fault, it is that the trumpets do not double the voice. But this is less of a concern here than it was in the Schumann because Wagner’s brass section is more evenly distributed among the chord tones. Whereas before, the brass instruments conflicted with the voice, strongly asserting one pitch while the voice asserted another, here the two sections — brass and voice — merely complement each other, neither conflicting nor agreeing unanimously.
