27 Echoing

To echo a tutti chord is to leave a few instruments playing after the tutti ends. The echo is often soft in dynamic, sounding like a natural reverberation (or “echo”) of the tutti it follows.

Echoes are typically:

  • complete chords
  • located in the middle register
  • closely spaced

Echoes usually share the same bass and soprano as the chord they are echoing:

Participating Instruments

Instruments do not need to have participated in a tutti in order to echo it. For example, an echoing instrument can enter on the last beat of the tutti, or even after the tutti ends.

If the tutti is loud enough, then the echoing instrument’s entrance will be disguised by the tutti’s reverb.

Gaps

Occasionally an echo will fill a gap in a tutti:

This is not ideal, but it is sometimes better than the alternatives, which include using a different soprano note, a wide spacing, or an incomplete chord in the echo.

Single Pitch & Intervallic Echoes

It is possible to echo a chord with a single pitch or interval. However, it is still ideal for such echoes to share the same pitch as the soprano and/or bass:
