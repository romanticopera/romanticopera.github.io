Adding a third to the octave creates an open sixth, a sonority particularly favored by Classical-era composers, including Beethoven, who ends his opera Fidelio with such an interval sustained over several measures:

By the 1850s, most tutti chords were comprised of full chords, with most of the spaces filled in, although the open sixth was still sometimes found in the highest register:

The sixth could be minor if the omitted note was the fifth of the chord (as above), or major if the omitted note was the root:

An open octave was also frequently heard at the top of chords:

Italian composers were particularly fond of this sonority, with the upper octave almost invariably being provided by the piccolo.

The Lowest Register

The lowest register, unlike the high and middle, almost always included open spaces, regardless of a composer’s national style. An “ideal” tutti chord featured a set of intervals descending in size from bottom to top in imitation of the harmonic series:

From these examples, the following principles can be derived: gaps are acceptable in the highest register and preferred in the lowest register. The density of the middle register depends on a chord’s quality and inversion, but in general the middle-register chord-tones are filled in by default.
