31 Conclusion

We have now learned many different principles governing the writing of tutti chords. To solidify this knowledge, we will look at one final tutti chord and analyze it for all the things we have learned. This final chord, appropriately enough, comes from Verdi’s final opera, Falstaff (p. 303):

Reduction:

Note the following:

  • The most prominent instruments (piccolo, trumpet, and first violins) double the voice
  • All the chord-tones in the middle register are filled in
  • Most doublings occur in the middle register
  • The flutes (and piccolo) play the highest notes of the chord
  • The oboes and clarinets play the chord-tones directly below the flutes
  • All the woodwind pairs play consonant intervals (M3 in the flutes, m6 in the oboes, m3 in the clarinets)
  • The horns leave a gap between themselves and the woodwinds, which is unusual, but the gap is filled by a trumpet, which is perfectly acceptable
  • The trombones fill in the chord-tones immediately below the trumpets
  • The bass trombone plays the bass
  • The timpani plays the bass
  • The root of the chord, C, and the melody note, E, are doubled more than the fifth
  • There are more roots than any other note in the chord:
    • C: 9
    • E: 6 (7 counting voice)
    • G: 5
  • Each section of the orchestra (woodwinds, brass, and strings) is playing a complete chord in its correct inversion (root-position)

All in all, this is a very consonant chord, and Verdi has emphasized its consonance in all the usual ways.
