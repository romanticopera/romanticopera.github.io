5 Triads in Root Position

Triads in root position are the most common of all tutti chords. They are also the most consonant. Composers often seek to emphasize this consonance in various ways, such as by writing consonant intervals for each woodwind pair and by doubling the root of the chord more than any other chord-tone.

If we look back at the Weber example from a few chapters ago, we see that the root of the chord is doubled three times by the various wind instruments while the third and fifth are doubled only once each:

Similarly, if we count up all the instances of each chord-tone, we find that there are more Cs in the chord than any other note:

  • C: 8
  • E: 4
  • G: 4

This further emphasizes the chord’s consonance.

Bizet’s chord fits our model in nearly every respect:

  • The flutes, oboes, and clarinets play the highest notes
  • The bassoons play the lowest notes
  • The horns fill in the chord-tones directly below the flutes, oboes, and clarinets
  • Each section of the orchestra (brass, woodwinds, and strings) has a complete chord in its correct inversion
  • The woodwind pairs play consonant intervals (m3 in the flutes, m3 in the clarinets)
  • The root (D) is doubled more than any other chord-tone (2 times vs. 0 for the third and fifth)
